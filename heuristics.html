List Of Heuristics

Here you will find an explanation of all 10 Heuristics, including examples relating to popular sites so you can see how exam answers are structured. Click on the modals to expand them and get more information. There are also embedded videos to help you learn. User Interface (UI) - The means by which the user and a computer system interact, in particular the use of input devices and software. The same basics of interface design are used across user interfaces to help users get to grips with things faster. It is a part of a broader user experience. A design is not usable or unusable by itself. It is the features together with what the user wants to do with it and the user's environment, that decide the usability. User Experience (UX) - The overall experience of a person using a product such as a website or computer application, especially in terms of how easy or pleasing it is to use.

  • Visibility Of System Status

  • “The design should always keep users informed about what is going on, through appropriate feedback within a reasonable amount of time.”

    When users know the current system status, they learn the outcome of their prior interactions and determine next steps. Predictable interactions create trust in the product as well as the brand. When creating apps and software, it's crucial to keep users informed about what's happening. Imagine using a social media app where you upload a photo but don't receive any feedback about whether it's uploading or finished. This lack of information can be frustrating because you're left wondering if your action was successful. Visibility of system status is important because it helps users stay informed about what's happening with their actions, reducing frustration and uncertainty while empowering them to make informed decisions and stay in control. On the Woolworths website, when you add items to your shopping cart, a notification pops up showing how many items you've added. This keeps you informed about what's in your cart at all times.

  • Match Between System And Real World

  • “The design should speak the users' language. Use words, phrases, and concepts familiar to the user, rather than internal jargon. Follow real-world conventions, making information appear in a natural and logical order.”

    In UX design, "Match Between the System and the Real World" means designing interfaces that use familiar language, concepts, and workflows that users already understand from their real-world experiences. This approach ensures that users can easily predict the system's behavior and confidently perform tasks without needing to learn new conventions. Importance: Reduced Cognitive Load: Users don't need to learn new ways of interacting with the interface because it mirrors their existing knowledge and experiences. Increased Usability: Interfaces that match real-world expectations are easier to navigate and understand, leading to higher user satisfaction. Intuitive Interactions: When users encounter familiar elements and behaviors, they can quickly accomplish tasks without confusion or frustration. Example: Woolworths Website The navbar has buttons with familiar icons that match what a user would do when shopping in person, with an icon of a shopping cart for the user’s cart, bags for choosing delivery options, and a magnifying glass for searching the site. The cart itself mimics a real-life shopping experience, where the user can look into the cart and see what they have added, and then take it directly to check out. This increases the usability of the site, because the process is intuitive for the user.

  • User Control and Freedom

  • “Users often perform actions by mistake. They need a clearly marked "emergency exit" to leave the unwanted action without having to go through an extended process.”

    User Control and Freedom is a key principle in design that ensures users feel in charge of their actions within a system. It emphasizes providing easy ways to undo actions, navigate freely, and make their own choices. This heuristic is important because it builds user confidence and prevents frustration, making the experience more enjoyable and intuitive. Implementing this might involve features like clear navigation options, an easy-to-find "back" button, and undo functionalities. For example, on the Woolworths website, if a user accidentally deletes items from their shopping cart, having an "Undo" button or an option to restore deleted items gives them the freedom to correct mistakes without hassle.

  • Consistency And Standards

  • “Users should not have to wonder whether different words, situations, or actions mean the same thing. Follow platform and industry conventions.”

    Consistency and Standards in design mean that users should encounter familiar elements and behaviors across a website, making it easier to navigate and understand. This heuristic is crucial because it reduces the learning curve and avoids confusion, as users can rely on established patterns. To implement this, designers ensure that buttons, fonts, colors, and layouts follow a uniform style, and common functions operate similarly throughout the site. On the Toyota website, for instance, consistent use of navigation menus, similar styles for car comparison tools, and standardized forms across different pages help users easily find information and perform tasks without needing to relearn how each part of the site works.

  • Error Prevention

  • “Good error messages are important, but the best designs carefully prevent problems from occurring in the first place. Either eliminate error-prone conditions, or check for them and present users with a confirmation option before they commit to the action.”

    Error Prevention in design focuses on anticipating potential mistakes users might make and designing the interface to help avoid these errors. This heuristic is essential because it saves users from frustration and ensures a smoother, more reliable experience. Implementing error prevention can involve features like confirming actions before they are final, providing helpful hints, and highlighting required fields in forms. On the PaknSave NZ website, for example, when users are placing an online grocery order, the site could have prompts that remind them if they've forgotten to select a delivery time or highlight any missing essential information before completing the purchase. This proactive approach helps users avoid common mistakes and ensures a more efficient shopping experience.

  • Recognition Rather Than Recall

  • “Minimize the user's memory load by making elements, actions, and options visible. The user should not have to remember information from one part of the interface to another. Information required to use the design (e.g. field labels or menu items) should be visible or easily retrievable when needed.”

    This UX design principle emphasizes making information and actions visible and easily accessible to users. Instead of relying on users' memory to recall information, the interface should prompt recognition of available options through clear cues and labels. Importance: Why it's Important: Ease of Use: Users can navigate and use the website more intuitively when options and actions are visible. Reduced Cognitive Load: Minimizing the need for users to remember information helps in reducing cognitive effort and frustration. Accessibility: It accommodates users of varying experience levels and familiarity with the platform. Trivago Example When the user goes to the search bar, they can recognise recently viewed hotels. This recognition can be based on the hotel's name, location, or even its image, without needing to actively recall specific details from memory. This familiarity helps users make decisions based on previous experiences or preferences without requiring them to remember all the details of the hotel.

  • Flexibility And Efficiency Of Use

  • “Shortcuts — hidden from novice users — may speed up the interaction for the expert user so that the design can cater to both inexperienced and experienced users. Allow users to tailor frequent actions.”

    This UX design principle focuses on providing options and features that accommodate both novice and expert users. It aims to enhance productivity by allowing users to choose the most efficient way to accomplish tasks based on their familiarity and skill level with the application. Why it's Important: User Empowerment: Users feel empowered when they can use the application in a way that suits their workflow and preferences. Productivity: Efficient tools and features enable users to complete tasks more quickly and effectively. Adaptability: Accommodating different skill levels ensures that both beginners and experienced users can utilize the application effectively. Example: Google Docs supports a wide range of keyboard shortcuts and commands that expert users can utilize to navigate, format text, insert elements (like tables and images), and collaborate more efficiently. Novice users can gradually learn and adopt these shortcuts to improve their efficiency over time. The search function is particularly useful for learning these keyboard shortcuts, because it shows the keys next to the function.

  • Aesthetic And Minimalistic Design

  • “Interfaces should not contain information that is irrelevant or rarely needed. Every extra unit of information in an interface competes with the relevant units of information and diminishes their relative visibility.“

    This principle focuses on creating interfaces that are visually appealing, uncluttered, and streamlined. It aims to present information and functionality in a clear and concise manner, enhancing user experience and usability. Aesthetic design elements can evoke positive emotions and make the interface more engaging for users. Minimalist design also reduces distractions and helps users focus on essential tasks. Overall, a intuitive and visually pleasing design improves usability and encourages users to interact with the platform more effectively. Example: The Woolworths website or app employs a clean layout with plenty of white space, allowing products and information to stand out clearly. This minimalist approach helps users navigate categories and find products easily. Important elements like product categories, search bar, and promotions are strategically placed and visually emphasized. This ensures that users can quickly locate what they are looking for without unnecessary visual clutter.

  • Help Users Recognise, Diagnose, And Recover From Errors

  • “Error messages should be expressed in plain language (no error codes), precisely indicate the problem, and constructively suggest a solution.“

    "Help Users Recognize, Diagnose, and Recover from Errors" is a key usability principle in user interface design. It ensures that users are informed when an error occurs, understand the nature of the error, and are guided towards resolving it. This principle is useful because it minimizes user frustration, enhances user satisfaction, and improves overall efficiency by reducing the time spent troubleshooting issues. For instance, if a user enters an incorrect password, a clear message like "Incorrect password. Please try again or reset your password" helps the user understand the mistake and offers a solution. Another example is when filling out an online form; if a required field is left blank, the interface should highlight the specific field and provide a message such as "This field cannot be left blank." On the Woolworths website, if a user attempts to checkout without selecting a delivery method, a helpful error message could be, "Please choose a delivery option to proceed with your order," along with a link or button to the delivery options page. This approach not only informs the user about the error but also guides them towards the necessary action to resolve it.

  • Help and Documentation

  • “It’s best if the system doesn’t need any additional explanation. However, it may be necessary to provide documentation to help users understand how to complete their tasks.“

    This UX design principle emphasizes providing users with accessible help resources and clear documentation to support their understanding and use of a product or feature. It aims to empower users with the information they need to effectively utilize the interface and resolve any uncertainties or issues that may arise. Importance: Accessible help resources enable users to learn and navigate the interface independently, fostering confidence and engagement. Clear documentation helps users troubleshoot issues, understand features, and maximize the utility of the product. Providing comprehensive help and documentation contributes to a positive user experience by reducing frustration and enhancing usability. Example: The Woolworths website has a section relating to how online shopping works, and how people can use the site. It has an explanation of how the site works, and how to order pick up. If that fails, Woolworths has a virtual assistant bot that can answer questions from the user in a friendly and personable manner.
